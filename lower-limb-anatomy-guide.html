Lower Limb Anatomy Guide

An interactive reference for the bones, muscles, nerves, and vasculature of the lower limb

Overview of the Lower Limb

The lower limb is designed for weight-bearing, locomotion, and maintaining balance. It is divided into the following regions:

Gluteal Region

The posterolateral area connecting the trunk to the free lower limb.

  • Contains the gluteal muscles (maximus, medius, minimus)
  • Deep lateral rotators of the hip
  • Superior and inferior gluteal neurovascular bundles
  • Sciatic nerve exits via the greater sciatic foramen

Thigh

The region between the hip and the knee, organized into three compartments.

  • Anterior: Quadriceps femoris, sartorius (femoral nerve)
  • Medial: Adductors of the hip (obturator nerve)
  • Posterior: Hamstrings (sciatic nerve)

Leg

The region between the knee and the ankle, also with three compartments.

  • Anterior: Dorsiflexors (deep fibular nerve)
  • Lateral: Fibularis longus & brevis (superficial fibular nerve)
  • Posterior: Plantarflexors (tibial nerve)

Foot

The terminal part of the lower limb, structured into arches for support.

  • Dorsum (top) and sole (plantar surface)
  • Medial longitudinal, lateral longitudinal, and transverse arches
  • Intrinsic muscles arranged in four plantar layers

Nerves of the Lower Limb

The lower limb is primarily supplied by the lumbar plexus (L1-L4) and the sacral plexus (L4-S3).

Clinical Correlations

Common Fractures

Fractures relevant to the lower limb.

  • Femoral neck fracture: Common in elderly; risks avascular necrosis due to disruption of medial circumflex femoral artery
  • Tibial shaft fracture: Most common long bone fracture; may cause compartment syndrome in the anterior compartment
  • Pott's fracture: Bimalleolar or trimalleolar ankle fracture from forced eversion
  • Jones fracture: Fracture at the base of the 5th metatarsal; poor blood supply delays healing

Nerve Injuries

Common nerve injuries and their presentations.

  • Femoral nerve injury: Loss of knee extension (quadriceps), decreased knee jerk reflex, numbness of anterior thigh & medial leg
  • Obturator nerve injury: Weakness of thigh adduction, sensory loss over medial thigh
  • Common fibular nerve injury: Foot drop (loss of dorsiflexion & eversion), steppage gait, sensory loss on dorsum of foot
  • Tibial nerve injury: Loss of plantarflexion & toe flexion, inability to stand on tiptoes, sensory loss on sole
  • Superior gluteal nerve injury: Trendelenburg gait (pelvis drops on unsupported side during stance phase)

Compartment Syndrome

A surgical emergency of the lower limb.

  • Most common in the anterior compartment of the leg
  • 5 P's: Pain (especially with passive stretch), Pressure, Pulselessness (late sign), Paresthesia, Paralysis (late sign)
  • Treatment: emergent fasciotomy
  • Can follow tibial fractures, crush injuries, or reperfusion

Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)

Thrombosis in the deep veins of the lower limb.

  • Virchow's triad: Stasis, endothelial injury, hypercoagulability
  • Most common in deep veins of the calf and thigh
  • Symptoms: unilateral leg swelling, warmth, erythema, calf tenderness
  • Risk: pulmonary embolism if thrombus dislodges
  • Diagnosis: compression ultrasound, D-dimer
